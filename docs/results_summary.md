# Results Summary

> ⚠️ **Synthetic data — methods demonstration only.** Every figure below was
> computed on fully synthetic claims generated by this repository, with a "true"
> effect deliberately built into the data generator. Nothing here is a real HPV
> vaccine-effectiveness estimate or a statement about any real product. The
> point is to show the analysis and the data-quality control working end to end.

## Executive summary

We ran an exposure-indexed, time-to-event analysis of HPV vaccination against
incident high-grade cervical lesions (CIN2+) / cervical cancer in a synthetic
insured population, and we ran an independent cross-source data-quality check on
the vaccination records.

- **Effectiveness (methods demo):** estimated vaccine effectiveness
  **VE ≈ 56% (95% CI ~29–72%)**, recovering the value baked into the generator
  (true VE = 55%). Crude and confounder-adjusted estimates agreed closely once
  exposure was handled as time-varying.
- **Data-quality finding:** the cross-source check **detected a systematic
  vaccine-classification error** affecting **1,669 registry dose records across
  1,288 patients (5.5% of patients with registry records)**, entirely within a
  single registry feed. This is the kind of finding that, in production, gets
  escalated to the data vendor before any product-specific result is released.

## Study at a glance

| Element | Value |
|---|---|
| Design | Observational, new-user / exposure-indexed cohort; time-to-event |
| Population | Adult women, ≥365 days continuous enrollment, no prior CIN2+/cancer |
| Cohort size | 9,850 members (after attrition; see `results/attrition_table.csv`) |
| Outcome | Incident CIN2+ / cervical cancer (ICD-10 N87.1, D06.9, C53.9) |
| Exposure | HPV vaccination (registry CVX + claims CPT + pharmacy NDC), time-varying |
| Estimate | Time-varying Cox; VE = (1 − HR) × 100% |

## What the analysis found

Vaccinated person-time had roughly **half** the incidence rate of unvaccinated
person-time (about 2.8 vs 6.3 events per 1,000 person-years), giving an adjusted
hazard ratio near **0.44** and VE near **56%**. Because exposure was modeled as
time-varying — unexposed person-time before vaccination, exposed after — the
estimate is not distorted by immortal-time bias, and adjustment for age, region,
and baseline cervical-screening intensity (the measured proxy for health-seeking
behavior) left the estimate essentially unchanged. The estimate sits right on
the generator's ground truth, which is the validation we wanted.

## The data-quality finding (the catch)

**What we saw.** Plotting registry doses over time by product showed Gardasil 9
(HPV9) doses recorded **before Gardasil 9 was available in the US (Dec 2014)** —
which is impossible. See `results/doses_over_time.png`.

**How we confirmed it.** Two independent checks agreed exactly:
1. **Internal plausibility** — the recorded `(dose_date, product)` pair fell
   outside the product's real US availability window.
2. **Cross-source reconciliation** — for the same patients, the *claims-derived*
   product (the administration CPT) did **not** corroborate the registry's
   product. Registry said Gardasil 9; the claims said an earlier product.

**Scope.** 1,669 dose records / 1,288 patients, **entirely confined to one feed
(the CA-style "CAIR2" registry)** — a hallmark of a feed-level processing error
rather than random noise.

**Impact.** The error is a *product misclassification*, not a missed dose: it
inflates apparent Gardasil 9 exposure and corrupts product-specific person-time.
So a **product-stratified** effectiveness estimate would be biased, while the
**all-product** ("any HPV vaccine") estimate is robust because the fact of
vaccination is unaffected. We would hold any product-specific result until the
feed is corrected.

## How I would escalate it (medical-affairs framing)

1. **Document and quantify** — share the reconciliation output
   (`results/qc_flagged_members.csv`, the over-time plot, the counts above) so
   the finding is concrete and reproducible, not anecdotal.
2. **Raise a data-provider query** with the registry vendor: a defined batch of
   CAIR2 records carries CVX 165 on pre-2014 dose dates; request root-cause
   (e.g., a code-mapping/back-fill error) and a corrected extract.
3. **Quarantine, don't delete** — flag the affected records in the analytic data
   set; never silently drop them.
4. **Sensitivity analysis** — re-run the (product-specific) analysis (a) excluding
   flagged records and (b) reclassifying them to the claims-implied product, and
   report whether conclusions change.
5. **Gate the deliverable** — release the all-product VE now (robust), and hold
   product-specific numbers pending the corrected feed; note the issue
   transparently in the study report.
6. **Prevent recurrence** — add this `(dose_date, product-availability)` plausibility
   check and the registry-vs-claims reconciliation as standing QC that runs on
   every refresh.

## Caveats

- **Synthetic data; methods demonstration only** — not a clinical or regulatory
  effectiveness claim.
- The descriptive Kaplan–Meier figure (`results/km_curve.png`) is by
  ever-vaccinated status and is subject to immortal-time caveats; the
  time-varying Cox model is the primary estimate.
- Death is treated as a censoring/competing event; a formal competing-risks
  analysis would be a natural extension.
